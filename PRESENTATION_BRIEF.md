12. What is your proposed answer or central argument?

    Answer: A useful plan does not assume that everything will work. It looks a
    few steps ahead, uses available data, and makes the reason behind each
    decision explicit: "I am doing this because of X." When reality changes,
    those reasons and assumptions can be reviewed instead of blindly following
    the original plan. The goal is not to celebrate or seek failure. It is to
    reduce avoidable failures and remain capable of responding to the failures
    that will eventually happen.

13. What are the three to five main points supporting that argument?

    Answer:

    - Think a few steps ahead, without pretending to predict everything.
    - Use data to support decisions, while recognizing that data is incomplete.
    - Know why an action or rule exists so it can be reconsidered when its
      assumptions change.
    - Challenge "why?", "should", and "should not."
    - Distinguish "we should not" from "we cannot." A convention, preference,
      or current limitation is not necessarily an impossibility.
    - Expect failures and changes, then adapt using what they teach you.
    - Treat behavior as part of professional competence. AI can help people
      implement concepts across different technologies, but it does not remove
      human responsibility for judgment, collaboration, trust, and consequences.

14. What common assumptions or misconceptions should be challenged?

    Answer: That good planning means predicting the future correctly; that a
    plan changing means the planning was bad; that established ways of working
    cannot be questioned; and that "we should not do this" means the same as
    "we cannot do this."

    A technical example: "Can I hardcode every password and secret in the
    source code because it is easier?" Yes, it is technically possible. Should
    I? No, because the short-term convenience creates security and maintenance
    risks. The important question is not only whether something can be done,
    but what consequences we are choosing by doing it.

    An everyday example: "Can I prepare an important school project on the last
    day?" Possibly. Should that be the plan? No, because it leaves no room for
    equipment failures, missing information, mistakes, or asking for help.
